BEASTLY BEARS
Numbers in Yellowstone:
- Approximately 150 with home ranges wholly or partially in park.
- As of 2016, 774 to 939 in Greater Yellowstone.
Where to See:
Dawn and dusk in the Hayden and Lamar valleys, on the north slopes of Mt. Washburn, and from Fishing Bridge to the East Entrance.
Size and Behavior:
- Males weigh 200 to 700 pounds, females weigh 200 to 400 pounds; adults stand about 3 feet at the shoulder.
- May live 15 to 30 years.
- Grizzly bears are generally 1 to 2 times larger than black bears of the same sex and age class within the same geographic region, and they have longer, more curved claws.
- Agile; can run up to 45 mph.
Status:
- The grizzly bear population in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem was returned to the federal threatened species list in 2009.
- Occupy 20,522 square miles in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem.
WILD WOLVES
Numbers in Yellowstone:
- About 500 wolves in the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem.
- As of December 2015, there were 115 wolves counted in the park, 54 in the northern range, and 61 in the interior. Ten packs were noted.
Where to See:
The northern range of Yellowstone is one of the best places in the world to watch wolves.
Size and Behavior:
- 26 to 36 inches tall at the shoulder, 4 to 6 feet long from nose to tail tip; males weigh 100 to 130 pounds, females weigh 80 to 110 pounds.
- Typically live 3 to 4 years in wild; can live up to 11 years in wild.
- Three color phases: gray is the most common; white is usually in the high Arctic; and black is common only in the Rockies.
- Prey primarily on hoofed animals. In Yellowstone, 90% of winter diet is elk; summer prey consist of more deer and smaller mammals.
DIVINE DEER
Numbers in Yellowstone:
- Summer: 1,850 to 1,900
- Winter: less than 400
Where to See:
- Summer: throughout the park
- Winter: North Entrance area
Size and Behavior:
- Male (buck): 150 to 250 pounds; female (doe): 100 to 175 pounds; 3 feet at the shoulder.
- Summer coat: reddish; winter coat: gray-brown;white rump patch with black-tipped tail; brown patch on forehead; large ears.
- Males grow antlers from April or May until August or September; shed them in late winter and spring.
- Lives in brushy areas, coniferous forests, grasslands.
BEAUTIFUL BIRDS
Numbers in Yellowstone:
- 285 documented species
- Approximately 150 species nest in the park.
Where to See:
- The variation in elevation and broad array of habitat types found within Yellowstone contribute to the relatively high diversity.
Current Management
- The Yellowstone National Park bird program monitors the park's bird species, including species of concern. The program's core activities are monitoring raptors (bald eagles, ospreys, peregrine falcons, golden eagles and red-tailed hawks), wetland birds, and passerine/near passerine birds (songbirds and woodpeckers).
Senstive Species
- Birds should be viewed from a distance. Getting too close can stress a bird (as it can any animal) and sometimes cause the bird to abandon its nest.
- The use of audio bird calls (or any other wildlife call) is illegal in the park.
- Never feed birds or other park wildlife.
